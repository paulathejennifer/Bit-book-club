    • Trevor Noah: Born to Be Great, In a World With No Chances

    Blogging: 25th April 2025

    Written by Karen Ngugi

    Why this title ?

    This name is a reflection of how, at the time, South African society forced children of two different races to degrade their worth and think of themselves as nothing, even though they could grow up to be impactful adults in the society.

    In "Born a Crime," Trevor Noah uses humour to highlight his painful upbringing as a mixed-race child in South Africa during the apartheid era. The narrative, which is satirical with a hint of humour, is essentially an eventful rollercoaster of his early experiences—both positive and negative—during and after apartheid. The primary focus of the book is Trevor's mother, Patricia Nambuyisola, and her perseverance in raising Trevor despite numerous unfavourable situations. Trevor, who was born to an African mother and a Swiss father, struggled because he was unable to identify with a particular race and had to spend most of his time in the shadows because it was against the law for a white person to have sex with a Black person. Trevor emphasises themes of poverty, segregation, violence, religion, and race, all of which highlight the difficulties of being Black, mixed-race, or coloured in South Africa during the apartheid era.

    He uses insightful life quotes from the various lessons he has learnt from his experiences to adorn his book. Among his well-known quotations was: "It’s so easy from the outside to put the blame on the woman and say, 'You just need to leave.' It’s not like my home was the only home where there was domestic abuse…. Where does a woman go in a society where that is the norm? Where the police won’t help her? When her own family won’t help her? Where does a woman go when she leaves one man who hits her and is just as likely to wind up with another man who hits her, maybe even worse than the first? Where does a woman go when she’s single with three kids and she lives in a society that makes her a pariah for being a manless woman? Where she’s seen as a whore for doing that? Where does she go? What does she do?" This was influenced by his mother being in an abusive marriage. Trevor's storytelling style demonstrates acceptance of life as it is and the ability to grow without giving up despite adversity.
